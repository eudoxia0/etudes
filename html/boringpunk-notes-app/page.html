4.5 Reaction mechanisms — SN1, SN2, E1, E2

Substrate, nucleophile, solvent, leaving group — decision tree for predicting products

The four workhorse mechanisms of introductory organic: two substitution paths (SN1 SN2) and two elimination paths (E1 E2). See also 4.1 Functional groups for leaving-group context and 4.3 Stereochemistry — R/S for the inversion / retention discussion.

★ Mnemonic
"1 = carbocation, 2 = concerted." The digit tells you the molecularity of the rate-determining step. Everything else (stereochemistry, kinetics, substrate preference) falls out of that single fact.

Overview & the 2×2 decision

Every time you see an alkyl halide (or similar substrate with a leaving group) react with a nucleophile/base, you're choosing between four possibilities on a 2×2 grid:

Unimolecular (1) Bimolecular (2)
Substitution (SN) SN1 via carbocation SN2 concerted, backside
Elimination (E) E1 via carbocation E2 concerted, anti-periplanar

The rate laws tell the story:

$$\text{rate}_{\mathrm{S_N1}} = k\,[\mathrm{R\text{-}LG}] \qquad \text{rate}_{\mathrm{S_N2}} = k\,[\mathrm{R\text{-}LG}]\,[\mathrm{Nu}^-]$$ (4.5.1)
$$\text{rate}_{\mathrm{E1}} = k\,[\mathrm{R\text{-}LG}] \qquad \text{rate}_{\mathrm{E2}} = k\,[\mathrm{R\text{-}LG}]\,[\mathrm{B}^-]$$ (4.5.2)

In words: the unimolecular reactions (SN1 E1) don't care how much nucleophile or base is around — they're gated by how fast the leaving group departs. The bimolecular reactions (SN2 E2) depend on both concentrations because bond-breaking and bond-making are concerted.

SN2 — concerted, backside attack

One-step: the nucleophile attacks the carbon bearing the leaving group from the opposite face, and the leaving group departs at the same instant. Inversion of configuration ("Walden inversion") is guaranteed at a chiral center.

Backside attack → Walden inversion Nu⁻ Nu ↓ │ H │ H H │ H \ │ / \ │ / \ │ / ──→ \ │ / C─LG C + LG⁻ / /│\ H H │ H (inverted)

Rate scales with substrate: methyl > 1° > 2° ≫ 3°. Tertiary substrates are essentially inert to SN2 — the three alkyl groups block the backside approach. Quantitatively, the Arrhenius-type rate dependence on steric bulk can be expressed as:

$$k_{\mathrm{S_N2}} \propto \exp\!\left(-\frac{E_a^{\ddagger} + \Delta E_{\text{steric}}}{RT}\right)$$ (4.5.3)

where the activation barrier $E_a^{\ddagger}$ gets an extra steric penalty $\Delta E_{\text{steric}}$ that rises sharply with substitution at the α-carbon. Favored by: strong nucleophile, polar aprotic solvent (DMSO, DMF, acetone), unhindered substrate.

SN1 — stepwise via carbocation

Two steps: (1) slow ionization to a carbocation; (2) fast nucleophilic capture. The carbocation is planar (sp²), so the nucleophile can attack either face → racemization at a chiral center (usually 50:50, sometimes skewed by ion-pair effects).

$$\mathrm{R\text{-}LG} \;\xrightarrow{\text{slow}}\; \mathrm{R^{+}} + \mathrm{LG^{-}} \;\xrightarrow{\text{fast},\,\mathrm{Nu}^{-}}\; \mathrm{R\text{-}Nu}$$ (4.5.4)

Carbocation stability is the entire ball game:

$$\mathrm{3^\circ} > \mathrm{2^\circ} \gg \mathrm{1^\circ} > \mathrm{CH_3^+}$$ (4.5.5)

Favored by: stabilized carbocation (3°, allylic, benzylic), polar protic solvent (water, alcohols — they solvate the ion pair), weak nucleophile. Note the direct inversion of the SN2 substrate preference — 3° substrates that refuse to undergo SN2 happily go SN1.

E2 — concerted anti-periplanar elimination

Base removes β-hydrogen as the leaving group departs, forming the π-bond in one step. Requires the H and LG to be anti-periplanar (dihedral ≈ 180°) for maximum orbital overlap.

Anti-periplanar transition state B:⁻ ↓ H │ │ │ B-H Cβ───Cα │ / \ ──→ Cβ═Cα + LG⁻ / \ / \ LG (H and LG at 180°) (π-bond formed)

Regiochemistry generally follows Zaitsev's rule — the more substituted alkene is more stable and dominates:

$$\text{stability: } R_2C{=}CR_2 > R_2C{=}CHR > R_2C{=}CH_2 > RHC{=}CHR > RHC{=}CH_2 > H_2C{=}CH_2$$ (4.5.6)

With bulky bases (e.g. t-BuO⁻), Hofmann's rule takes over and the less-substituted alkene dominates (steric access to the less-hindered β-H).

E1 — stepwise via carbocation

Same first step as SN1 (slow ionization to carbocation), then base abstracts a β-hydrogen. Because it's the same carbocation intermediate, SN1 and E1 are always competitors — you'll get a mixture, with temperature favoring E1 (entropy) and nucleophile strength favoring SN1.

The product ratio can be estimated from the Eyring equation applied to the second-step transition states:

$$\frac{[\text{alkene}]}{[\text{substitution product}]} \;=\; \exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta\Delta G^{\ddagger}_{\text{E1}-\text{S_N1}}}{RT}\right)$$ (4.5.7)

At higher $T$, the $T\Delta S^{\ddagger}$ contribution favors the elimination path (one molecule becomes two, $\Delta S^{\ddagger} > 0$), which is the thermodynamic argument behind the classical lab rule: "heat favors elimination."

Worked example: 2-bromo-2-methylbutane + methanol

Given (CH₃)₂C(Br)CH₂CH₃ (tertiary alkyl bromide) dissolved in methanol at room temperature. Predict products.

Analysis:

  1. Substrate is 3° → rules out SN2 (steric block at α-carbon).
  2. Solvent is polar protic (MeOH) and methanol is a weak nucleophile / weak base → rules out E2.
  3. Remaining options: SN1 and E1, both via the same tertiary carbocation.
$$(\mathrm{CH_3})_2\mathrm{C(Br)CH_2CH_3} \;\xrightarrow{\text{MeOH}}\; (\mathrm{CH_3})_2\mathrm{C^{+}CH_2CH_3} + \mathrm{Br^{-}}$$ (4.5.8)

From the carbocation, two pathways:

  • SN1 product: MeOH attacks C⁺ → 2-methoxy-2-methylbutane (after deprotonation)
  • E1 product: MeOH (as a weak base) removes β-H → 2-methyl-2-butene (Zaitsev, trisubstituted) as major, plus minor 2-methyl-1-butene.

Typical ratio at room temperature: ~60:40 SN1:E1. Heat the reaction and the ratio flips — consistent with equation (4.5.7) and the positive $\Delta S^{\ddagger}$ for elimination.

Decision heuristic (for exam)

★ 4-step decision
  1. Substrate class? Methyl/1° → SN2 almost always. 3° → never SN2.
  2. Strong base/nucleophile present? Yes → bimolecular (SN2 or E2). No → unimolecular (SN1 / E1).
  3. Substitution or elimination? Bulky base → E. Good nucleophile, unhindered C → S.
  4. Temperature check: high T tips everything toward elimination.
